Module 1 · Foundation

How the financial system works

A mental model of the whole system before you zoom into the details — because your data pipelines will cross every layer of this.

🔧 Data engineer framing You've built pipelines. Finance is a world where every event generates a record — a trade, a payment, a price tick, a compliance report. Your job is to move, model, and serve that data reliably. Start by understanding what generates it.

The big picture: who needs money, who has money

At its core, finance solves a single problem: connecting people who need capital with people who have capital. A startup needs £10M to grow. A pension fund has £10M sitting idle. Finance is the plumbing between them.

This happens through two main channels:

  • Capital markets — companies sell stocks or bonds directly to investors
  • Banking — banks take deposits and lend them out

The three big sectors you'll encounter

Retail & commercial banking

Banks take deposits from individuals and businesses and lend that money out as mortgages, business loans, credit cards etc. The difference between what they earn on loans and what they pay on deposits is the net interest margin — the main profit driver.

For data engineers, banks are a goldmine of transactional data: every payment, every ATM withdrawal, every transfer generates a record.

-- Key tables you'll likely work with in banking
TRANSACTIONS -- every debit/credit event
ACCOUNTS -- current, savings, loan accounts
CUSTOMERS -- KYC-verified customer records
PRODUCTS -- mortgages, credit cards, etc.
PAYMENTS -- SWIFT, SEPA, Faster Payments

Asset management

Asset managers (BlackRock, Vanguard, Fidelity etc.) pool money from many investors and invest it on their behalf. They charge a fee — typically a % of assets under management (AUM).

Key distinction: buy side (asset managers buy securities) vs sell side (banks sell/trade securities). You'll hear this constantly.

-- Key tables in asset management
PORTFOLIOS -- each fund or mandate
HOLDINGS -- what each portfolio owns
TRADES -- buy/sell orders executed
BENCHMARKS -- e.g. FTSE 100, S&P 500
PERFORMANCE -- returns vs benchmark

Insurance

Insurers collect premiums and pay out claims. The money between premium and payout is invested — so insurers are also major asset managers. The key concept is the float: money collected but not yet paid out as claims.

-- Key tables in insurance
POLICIES -- active insurance contracts
CLAIMS -- filed and settled claims
PREMIUMS -- payments received
UNDERWRITING -- risk assessment data

Capital markets

This is where securities (stocks, bonds, derivatives) are issued and traded. Investment banks (Goldman, JPMorgan, Barclays) sit at the centre — they underwrite new issues and make markets.

Data here is ultra high-volume and time-sensitive. A single trading day can generate billions of rows.

-- Key tables in capital markets
ORDERS -- intent to buy/sell
EXECUTIONS -- fills — actual trades done
POSITIONS -- net holding by desk/book
MARKET_DATA -- price ticks, OHLCV
RISK_METRICS -- VaR, Greeks, exposures

Why this matters for your data work

Data engineer perspective Finance is one of the most data-intensive industries on earth. The London Stock Exchange alone processes millions of messages per second. A single transaction at a bank can trigger 40+ downstream records — in compliance systems, GL, risk systems, reporting. You will constantly be asked "why doesn't this number match?" — understanding the domain is how you answer that.

Financial instruments

What gets bought, sold, and valued — and what fields you'll see in the data for each.

🔧 Why this matters Every instrument has different fields, settlement rules, and pricing conventions. When you join a trade table to a security master, you need to know if you're dealing with a bond (has maturity + coupon) or an equity (has dividends + splits). Getting this wrong breaks P&L calculations.

The instrument hierarchy

All financial instruments fall into two fundamental categories: cash instruments (you own the thing) and derivatives (you own a contract based on the thing).

Key instruments explained

Click any card to expand it.

Equity (Stock / Share) Instrument

Ownership stake in a company. When you buy a share of Apple, you own a tiny fraction of Apple. The price moves based on supply and demand — which is driven by expectations of future profits.

Key concepts: market cap = share price × shares outstanding. Dividends are periodic cash distributions. Corporate actions (splits, rights issues) affect holdings data.

Data engineer note Equities are identified by ticker (AAPL) + exchange, but also by ISIN or CUSIP globally. Watch out for ticker reuse — the same ticker can mean a different company after a corporate action. Always join on a stable ID like ISIN.
Bond (Fixed Income) Instrument

A loan you make to a government or company. They pay you a fixed interest rate (the coupon) periodically, and repay the face value (principal) at maturity. Price moves inversely to interest rates — when rates rise, bond prices fall.

Key concepts: Yield = effective return if held to maturity. Duration = sensitivity to interest rate changes. Credit rating (AAA → junk) indicates default risk.

Data engineer note Bonds have more fields than equities: maturity_date, coupon_rate, coupon_frequency, day_count_convention, first_coupon_date. The day_count_convention (Actual/360, 30/360, etc.) affects interest calculations — it's a notorious source of bugs.
Derivative (Futures & Options) Instrument

A contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset. You don't own the underlying — you own a right or obligation related to it.

Future: obligation to buy/sell at a set price on a set date. Used to hedge or speculate.

Option: right (but not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) at a set price (strike) by a set date (expiry).

Data engineer note Derivatives have extra fields: underlying_id, strike_price, expiry_date, option_type (call/put), notional, multiplier. Expiry dates mean records become inactive — you need to handle this in your slowly changing dimensions. Derivatives also generate daily mark-to-market P&L even without trades.
Swap Instrument

An agreement to exchange cash flows with a counterparty. The most common is an interest rate swap (IRS): one party pays fixed interest, the other pays floating (e.g. SONIA + spread). Used to manage interest rate risk.

Data engineer note Swaps are OTC (over-the-counter) — bilateral agreements, not exchange-traded. They're reported to trade repositories (post-2008 regulation). The data includes notional, fixed_rate, floating_rate_index, payment_frequency, start_date, end_date. Generating cash flow schedules from these fields is a common data engineering task.
Fund / ETF / Index Instrument

A pooled vehicle holding many instruments. A mutual fund is priced once daily. An ETF (Exchange Traded Fund) trades on an exchange like a stock. An index (like the FTSE 100) is not directly investable — it's a benchmark.

Data engineer note Funds have a NAV (Net Asset Value) calculated end-of-day. Holdings change daily. You'll often need to "look through" a fund to see its underlying securities for risk calculations. Watch for circular holdings (fund A holds fund B which holds fund A).

Markets & trading venues

Where instruments are traded, how prices are formed, and what data gets generated.

🔧 Data engineer framing Different venues produce different data formats and latencies. Understanding venue types tells you why your pipeline might receive FIX protocol messages from one source and CSV end-of-day files from another.

Exchange vs OTC

Trading happens in two fundamentally different environments:

FeatureExchange (on-venue)OTC (off-exchange)
WhereCentralised: LSE, NYSE, EuronextBilateral, directly between parties
TransparencyPublic order book, pre/post-trade dataPrivate — reported after the fact
InstrumentsEquities, futures, listed options, ETFsBonds, swaps, FX, bespoke derivatives
ClearingCentral counterparty (CCP) like LCHBilateral or via CCP post-2012
Data formatFIX protocol, binary (ITCH, PITCH)Proprietary, XML, trade repos

Order book mechanics

On an exchange, all buy and sell orders are aggregated into a limit order book (LOB). The best bid (highest buy offer) and best ask (lowest sell offer) form the bid-ask spread — the cost of immediacy.

Order book snapshot (data representation)

A level 2 order book gives you depth:

BID side: £98.50 × 1000 | £98.45 × 2500 | £98.40 × 800
ASK side: £98.55 × 500 | £98.60 × 1500 | £98.65 × 3000
Spread = 98.55 - 98.50 = 5p

L1 data = just best bid/ask. L2 = full depth. L3 = individual orders. Your data infrastructure decisions will often hinge on which level you need.

Market data types you'll work with

Tick data Data type

Every individual price change, at nanosecond resolution. This is the rawest, highest-volume data. A busy stock might have millions of ticks per day. Stored in specialist time-series databases (KDB+, Arctic, TimescaleDB).

OHLCV (bars) Data type

Aggregated tick data: Open, High, Low, Close, Volume for a given period (1min, 1h, 1d). Far more manageable for backtesting and analytics. This is what most people mean by "market data" in a reporting context.

Data engineer note OHLCV bars should always be adjusted for corporate actions (dividends, splits). Unadjusted vs adjusted prices are a common reconciliation issue. Store both versions and flag which is which.
Reference / static data Data type

Security master: issuer, instrument type, currency, exchange listing, identifiers, corporate actions history. This data changes slowly but is critical — errors here cascade everywhere.

Data engineer note Reference data is a SCD (Slowly Changing Dimension) Type 2 problem. When a company changes its ticker or does a merger, you need to preserve history. Maintain effective_from and effective_to dates.

Other key participants you'll encounter

Central bank Market

Bank of England, ECB, Federal Reserve. Sets monetary policy (interest rates), acts as lender of last resort, issues currency. Their decisions move every market. Key rate: the base rate / fed funds rate. Your pipelines will often ingest their rate decisions as events.

Central counterparty (CCP) Market

LCH Clearnet, ICE Clear. Stands between buyer and seller, guaranteeing the trade. After 2008, regulators required most derivatives to be centrally cleared. CCPs require margin (collateral) from both sides.

Data engineer note Margin calls (daily cash movements to/from CCP) generate their own data flows. Initial margin (IM) and variation margin (VM) are calculated and reported daily.
Custodian Market

State Street, BNY Mellon, HSBC Custody. Holds securities on behalf of asset managers. They also provide fund administration (calculating NAV), reporting, and securities lending services.

Data engineer note Custodians send daily position files (usually SWIFT messages or CSV). Reconciling your internal positions against custodian statements is a core back-office data task — breaks must be investigated.
Data vendor Data type

Bloomberg, Refinitiv (LSEG), FactSet, ICE Data Services. They aggregate and distribute market data, reference data, and analytics. You'll spend a lot of time building pipelines to ingest their feeds.

Data engineer note Bloomberg has its own terminal and API (BLPAPI). Their data is expensive and access-controlled. A key job is building a golden copy of reference data from multiple vendor sources, resolving conflicts with a priority order.

Critical data patterns

1. Never use float for money

Floating point cannot represent decimal values exactly. 0.1 + 0.2 = 0.30000000000000004 in IEEE 754. Use:

-- In your DDL
amount DECIMAL(19, 6) -- or NUMERIC
price DECIMAL(28, 10) -- bond prices can have many decimals
quantity DECIMAL(18, 0) -- whole shares usually
fx_rate DECIMAL(18, 10) -- currency pairs need precision

-- In Python, use: decimal.Decimal, not float

2. Business date vs calendar date

Finance has its own calendars. A trade on Friday settles on the next business day (Monday), not Saturday. "T+2" means 2 business days, skipping weekends and public holidays. Each exchange has its own holiday calendar.

-- Tables you'll likely need
HOLIDAY_CALENDAR
  calendar_id VARCHAR(10) -- e.g. 'LON', 'NYC', 'TOK'
  holiday_date DATE
  description VARCHAR(100)

3. Bi-temporal data (as-of + as-at)

This is the most important pattern in finance data. Every piece of data has two time dimensions:

DimensionMeaningSQL column
Valid time (as-of)When was this true in the real world?valid_from, valid_to
Transaction time (as-at)When did we know/record this?created_at, updated_at
🔧 Example A trade is booked on Monday (transaction_time=Monday) but dated Friday (valid_time=Friday). Risk reports run "as of" Friday need to include it. Audit reports run "as at" Monday might not. Getting this wrong causes reconciliation breaks.

4. Currency handling

Every money value must travel with its currency code. For reporting, you'll need FX rates to convert to base currency. Always store both the original currency and the converted amount:

TRADE
  notional_local DECIMAL(19,6)
  currency_local CHAR(3) -- ISO 4217: GBP, USD, EUR
  notional_base DECIMAL(19,6)
  currency_base CHAR(3)
  fx_rate DECIMAL(18,10)
  fx_rate_date DATE -- WHICH rate used?
Module 6 · Risk & Compliance

Risk management

Risk is the core business of finance. Every firm measures, manages, and reports it — and needs data engineers to make that possible.

🔧 Data engineer framing Risk metrics are computed from your data. If your trades table has bad data, the risk number is wrong. Risk systems are often the most critical downstream consumers of your pipelines.

Types of risk

Market risk Risk

Risk of loss from market price movements: equity prices falling, interest rates rising, FX rates moving. Measured by:

  • VaR (Value at Risk) — max loss over N days at X% confidence. E.g. "1-day 99% VaR = £1M" means there's a 1% chance of losing more than £1M tomorrow.
  • Greeks (for options) — Delta, Gamma, Vega, Theta measure sensitivity to different market factors.
  • PnL (P&L) — daily profit and loss by position, desk, book.
Data engineer note VaR requires historical price data (typically 250 days) and position data. You'll build pipelines to feed risk engines (Murex, Calypso, custom quant libraries) with both. Results are stored in risk_metrics tables with as_of_date and run_type (end_of_day, intraday).
Credit risk Risk

Risk that a counterparty won't pay what they owe. A bank lending money might not get it back. Measured by:

  • PD (Probability of Default) — how likely is this borrower to default?
  • LGD (Loss Given Default) — if they default, how much do we lose?
  • EL = PD × LGD × EAD — expected loss formula (EAD = exposure at default)
  • CVA — Credit Valuation Adjustment on derivatives
Data engineer note Credit risk models need counterparty data, collateral data, exposure data, and credit ratings. Building a counterparty hierarchy (subsidiaries of a parent) for aggregate exposure is a classic data engineering problem.
Operational risk Risk

Risk from failed processes, systems, or people. Fraud, data errors, system outages, human error. Basel II/III requires capital to be held against it. Measured via loss event databases and scenario analysis.

Data engineer note You are part of the operational risk story. A data pipeline that fails, corrupts data, or produces late reports is an operational risk event. Data quality monitoring is risk management.
Liquidity risk Risk

Risk that a firm can't meet its cash obligations. Two types: market liquidity (can't sell a position without moving the price) and funding liquidity (can't raise cash when needed). The 2008 crisis was largely a liquidity crisis.

Regulators require banks to hold liquid assets via LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio) and NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio).

Module 7 · Risk & Compliance

Regulations & compliance

The rules that govern finance — and the reporting infrastructure you'll build to satisfy them.

🔴 Critical for data engineers Regulatory reporting is non-negotiable. Fines for late or inaccurate reports are enormous (billions). Data quality in compliance pipelines must be near-perfect. This is high-stakes engineering.

The regulatory landscape

Pre-2008
Light regulation era
Banks largely self-regulate. Basel I/II set capital requirements. Financial reporting standards (IFRS, GAAP) apply.
2008
Global financial crisis
Lehman Brothers collapses. $700B US bailout. Regulators realise they had no visibility into systemic risk. A wave of regulation follows.
2010–2015
Post-crisis regulation wave
Dodd-Frank (US), EMIR (EU), Basel III, MiFID II — a flood of new reporting requirements creating massive demand for financial data infrastructure.
2018–now
Data & privacy overlay
GDPR (data privacy), BCBS 239 (data risk principles), ESG reporting requirements. Finance data engineering becomes a critical function.

Key regulations you'll encounter

MiFID II Compliance

Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II — EU regulation governing trading and transparency. Requires firms to report every trade in detail, with timestamps accurate to microseconds for high-frequency trading. Also covers best execution (proving trades were done at best available price).

Came into force: January 2018. UK kept an equivalent (UK MiFIR) post-Brexit.

Data engineer note MiFID II creates two main reporting obligations: transaction reporting (to regulator within T+1) and trade reporting (to an Approved Reporting Mechanism, ARM). You'll build pipelines that generate these reports, validate them, and manage rejections. A single rejected field can invalidate the whole report.
EMIR Compliance

European Market Infrastructure Regulation — requires reporting of all derivatives contracts to a Trade Repository (TR). Also requires central clearing of standardised derivatives. This came directly from G20 commitments post-2008 to improve OTC market transparency.

Data engineer note EMIR reporting requires a UTI (Unique Trade Identifier) on every trade — generated and shared between counterparties. Pairing UTIs between counterparties is an ongoing reconciliation task. Reports go daily to TRs like DTCC or REGIS-TR in XML format.
Basel III / IV Compliance

Global banking standards from the Basel Committee (BIS). Prescribes how much capital banks must hold relative to their risk-weighted assets (RWA). The more risk, the more capital required as a buffer. Also includes liquidity requirements (LCR, NSFR).

Data engineer note Capital calculation is a massive data exercise. Every loan, position, and exposure must be risk-weighted using standardised or internal models. The COREP (Common Reporting) templates are the EU's Basel reporting format — hundreds of fields submitted quarterly.
GDPR in finance Compliance

General Data Protection Regulation — not finance-specific, but profoundly impacts financial data. Customers can request their data (Subject Access Request, SAR), request deletion (right to erasure), and data must be used only for stated purposes. Financial data often has long retention requirements (7 years for AML purposes) which creates tension with the right to erasure.

Data engineer note You'll need to build data lineage to handle SARs (what data do we hold about this customer, across all systems?). Deletion requests create problems in immutable audit logs — the resolution is often pseudonymisation rather than true deletion. Tag PII columns in your catalogue from day one.
BCBS 239 Compliance

Principles for Effective Risk Data Aggregation and Risk Reporting — this one is directly about data. It requires banks to be able to aggregate risk data accurately and quickly, even in a crisis. 14 principles covering data architecture, governance, and reporting.

Data engineer note BCBS 239 is basically a regulatory mandate for good data engineering. Principle 6 requires data to be "accurate and reliable". Principle 7 requires completeness. Many banks have failed BCBS 239 audits due to poor data lineage, manual processes, and siloed systems. This is your domain.
AML / KYC Compliance

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) — requirements to verify customer identity and monitor for suspicious activity. Firms must file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) to national financial intelligence units (e.g., UK NCA).

Data engineer note AML pipelines are high-stakes ML workloads — pattern detection on transaction flows. Transaction monitoring systems (Actimize, NICE, Temenos) need clean, timely transaction feeds. False positive rates matter: too many alerts → analysts overwhelmed. Data quality directly affects compliance outcomes.

Recurring engineering problems in finance

Position reconciliation Pattern

Every day, your internal system's position (what you think you own) must match what the custodian says you own. Breaks = differences. Finding and resolving breaks is a core daily operation.

-- Break detection query
SELECT i.security_id, i.quantity as internal_qty,
  c.quantity as custodian_qty,
  (i.quantity - c.quantity) as break_qty
FROM internal_positions i
FULL OUTER JOIN custodian_positions c
  USING(security_id, portfolio_id, as_of_date)
WHERE i.quantity != c.quantity OR i.quantity IS NULL
End-of-day batch processing Pattern

After markets close, a complex dependency chain runs: get final prices → mark positions to market → calculate P&L → run risk → generate compliance reports → feed accounting. All before the next trading day begins. Failure in any step cascades.

Data engineer note EOD batch timing is brutal. If the US equity close is 9pm UK time, your pipeline window to produce reports before 7am might be under 10 hours. Optimise for restartability — each step should be idempotent. Use a run_date partition key everywhere.
Corporate action processing Pattern

When a company splits its stock 2:1, pays a dividend, or is acquired, all holdings need to be adjusted. Getting this wrong means your historical P&L calculations are broken — permanently, unless you replay history.

Data engineer note Corporate actions arrive from data vendors (Bloomberg, Refinitiv) and must be validated, applied to positions and price history, and audited. This is one of the most error-prone areas in financial data. Always keep unadjusted prices as well as adjusted.
Regulatory report generation Pattern

Assembling, validating, and submitting MiFID II transaction reports, EMIR trade reports, capital reports (COREP), liquidity reports (FINREP) to regulators on strict schedules. Reports are often in XML or ISO 20022 format.

Data engineer note Build a report generation layer separate from your core data model. Include validation (check every required field, check permitted values, check date logic) before submission. Log every submission and response. Build automated rejection handling — regulators send rejection codes you must resolve and resubmit.
